# Challenge (top-down, v0 DRAFT — for review) — assemble a working minimal copier from the far end

*Draft complement to [`CHALLENGE-fold.md`](CHALLENGE-fold.md). **Same gate-6 target, opposite direction.** The folding challenge builds UP from a uniform local rule and keeps hitting the capacity wall; this one starts at the FINISH LINE — the known landscape of minimal, non-nucleic-acid copiers — and works BACK to the least architecture that copies. The two are a pincer: they should meet at gate 6. **Not yet a live outbound; drafted for inspection.** Read [`CLAUDE.md`](CLAUDE.md) (the guards) first — Guard 0 (constructive, not forensic) and the n=1 rule bite hardest on this side.*

## The setup — you are given the top, not the primitives

A substantial body of work has built objects that copy **without** RNA/DNA machinery. They define what a working copier IS (its function), not how life happened:

- **Mechanical / geometric replicators** — Penrose's plywood self-reproducing units (1950s); programmable self-assembly (Winfree; Griffith; Schulman).
- **Colloidal self-replication** — patchy-particle clusters that copy a target geometry (Zeravcic & Brenner, 2014) — heredity carried by *shape*, TP's own register.
- **Minimal molecular self-replicators** — the autocatalytic-template law and its √-kinetics (von Kiedrowski), realized non-nucleically in Rebek's self-complementary replicators.
- **Peptide self-replicators** — Ghadiri's autocatalytic peptide ligation; Ashkenasy's replicator networks — copying with no nucleic acid at all.
- **Collectively autocatalytic sets** — Kauffman; Hordijk–Steel RAF theory — copying as a *network* property, not a molecule.
- **Fueled / dissipative self-assembly** — Boekhoven; van Esch; Prins — the drive-half: structures that persist only while fuel flows.

These are the "other side." **Working backward from a functioning copier**, give the minimal **assembly account** below.

## The target (shared with the folding challenge)

The assembled object must copy itself with fidelity that is **discrete & templated**, **carried by the drive** (vanishes cold), **encoded in the copied structure** (so a better copier copies itself better), **heritable**, and **above the Eigen bar** `N·(1−q) < 1`. The crux is identical: **where does improvable, structure-carried fidelity come from?** The only new freedom is direction — you may *assume a working copier exists and decompose it*, rather than build one up from a uniform local rule.

## Deliverable — the assembly account

1. **Parts list.** The minimal components, each justified as *functionally necessary* by the copying sciences (Shannon capacity; von Neumann constructor–tape; Hopfield–Ninio proofreading; Eigen) — never by resemblance to extant biology. For each part: state what fails if you remove it.
2. **Couplings.** How the parts interact so the whole templates a copy.
3. **Assembly logic.** How the parts are brought together into a working copier — as an *engineering procedure in a substrate, on demand*. Say what does the assembling; no external intelligent hand, and no appeal to when/why it arose.
4. **The fidelity-capacity account — the crux.** Name the component that supplies *improvable* fidelity, and show it is carried by the **copied structure + the drive**, not by a dedicated external machine. State it concretely enough to hand to the bottom-up track: **either as a local, uniform rule, or by naming exactly the non-local / privileged element it requires.**
5. **The minimal-machinery verdict.** State honestly the least privileged machinery a working copier needs: a dedicated catalyst? a processive element? none? **"This much dedicated machinery is forced" is a legitimate and valuable answer** — it is the gate-6 price the bottom-up track keeps circling without naming.

## Guard rails (the account is void if it breaks these)

- **No frozen accidents.** Do not read extant DNA/RNA polymerase architecture backward as necessity. One contingent, optimized solution is not a law (n=1). Justify every part by *what copying requires*, not by what evolved.
- **Constructive, not forensic.** "How it assembles" = how you would build it, on demand, in a substrate — not when, why, or how often it arose. Drop any thread that turns to Earth's history.
- **Substrate-general.** State the account functionally; a lattice / sphere-cap / colloidal / peptide realization is an *instance*, not the claim.
- **Meet the bottom-up floor.** For the account to be a *target* and not a wish, its fidelity mechanism must be realizable by local, uniform dynamics — or it must *name* the non-local component it needs (which becomes the measured chemistry price). An account whose "copier" is an unexplained machine that reads the tape has not solved gate 6; it has only relocated it.

## What makes an account good (how it will be read)

It is an **argument, not a mechanical witness** — so it cannot *close* gate 6 the way an explicit `W` could; it *defines the finish line*, and at best imports the answer from systems that demonstrably work. (That is its compensating strength over a built toy: it is anchored to real copiers, not rigged to pass an oracle.) A good account:

- **(a)** every part is functionally necessary — removing it names a *specific* failure;
- **(b)** the fidelity-capacity mechanism is explicit and structure+drive-carried, stated concretely enough to hand down to the folding track;
- **(c)** it states its minimal machinery honestly — including "some dedicated machinery is forced," if that is the truth;
- **(d)** it is grounded in ≥1 real system from the landscape above, not invented from scratch.

The payoff is a **blueprint the folding track builds toward.** If the top-down account's fidelity mechanism turns out to be realizable by a uniform local rule, that is the witness the bottom-up track could not find. If it is not — if a dedicated catalyst / processive element is genuinely forced — that is the honest gate-6 answer, reached from the top. Either way, **the pincer closes the gate where the two directions meet.**
