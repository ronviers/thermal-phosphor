## Bottom line — a clean, in-discipline **negative** (with the rung located)

A driven, layered, mass-carried medium **can** copy a discrete **stacking-order Z₂** state (fcc ABCABC vs hcp ABAB) across a separation, by contact, and keep it discrete through the pinch. It gets **one rung further than chirality**. But it **fails the gate that matters — strong closure**: the copying fidelity *q* is set by the **bath** (supersaturation, temperature, the dislocation line), **not by the sequence**, so the copier is **off the tape**. Pressure can **sort** a standing population; it cannot **store** improvement on the copied object. That is §6's own predicted failure mode (*"pressure sorts, it does not store"*) and it is **Cairns-Smith's clay-gene failure** exactly (the direct precedent, model c). **Pure geometry / compass does not close.**

## The one refinement the canon must absorb — weak vs strong closure

STATE.md §6 uses "closure" for two different things and asserts stacking has both:

- **Weak closure** — *"output-is-input, tape and read-head are one object, no adapter."* Stacking **has** this (a granular layer templates the next with no separate read-head — model c flags it as where stacking *beats* systems that need a polymerase). *Caveat: this is the generous read; whether a fully **detached** daughter row templates without a growth front is itself part of the open question, so treat weak-closure as "arguably passes," not settled.*
- **Strong closure** — *"the gate's quality lives on the tape, so a better-copying variant copies itself better."* Stacking **lacks** this. This is the bootstrap; it is what §6 needs; it is what fails.

§6 line 119: *"everything upstream is buildable in the head with a compass; that gate is the transistor to squint at."* **The squint's answer: the transistor needs chemistry, not just a compass.** And since stacking's copier is off-tape, the stacking pivot **does not by itself clear §4's analog-mold ceiling** — it is the same *sorts-not-stores* class. Fold this into the §4/§5/§6 cleanup pass.

## The rescue path (the live frontier now)

Named by model c: make the **stacking-fault energy itself sequence-encoded** — *patchy* particles / sticks whose **binding affinity depends on local stacking parity**, so a variant with a stronger correct-patch improves its own *q*. That puts *q* on the tape and would close the loop. But model c is blunt: that is *"stacking plus chemistry — essentially inventing base-pairing."* The open, TP-shaped question is whether there is a **minimal** such addition that puts *q* on the tape **without** amounting to full base-pairing — i.e. how much chemistry is *forced*, and whether it can still be read as "a compass plus one affinity bit" rather than "import DNA." **This is what the next outbound round should ask** — not a reconfirmation of the (solid) negative.

## Appendix — the two substantive answers, verbatim

### Model b

> **Status check:** I swept the outbound multi-model research channel and public repositories. There is no existing report matching this prompt or the `thermal-phosphor` architecture queued for July 2026. We are clear to synthesize a fresh response.
>
> Here is the honest, bottom-line verdict: **It is a clean negative.**
>
> While stacking-order (and topological mechanics) can absolutely provide a discrete, metastable state that survives separation and copies by contact, **they fail the closure requirement.** The fidelity of the copying process lives in the physics of the growth medium—the "hardware"—not in the sequence itself. Because the tape cannot modify its own replication machinery, it cannot bootstrap its way out of the Eigen error threshold.
>
> **Q1 & Q4 — Discreteness, Metastability, Separation (the good news).** *Established and highly viable.* Stacking order is genuinely discrete at the growth front — an incoming layer sits in specific crystallographic hollows (A/B/C), no continuous smear; the geometry forces a discrete Z₂ choice. *Metastability* is governed by the kinetic barrier to sliding: the thermodynamic ΔE (stacking-fault energy) is often a few meV/atom, but moving a deposited layer requires a large collective kinetic barrier — once clicked into place it is locked. *Separation* easily survives detachment: cleaving/exfoliating along the basal plane strictly preserves the sequence; intra-layer bonds ≫ inter-layer forces.
>
> **Q2 — Copy-by-contact.** *Established, but purely thermodynamic.* A set layer templates a blank layer against it (2D nucleation epitaxy). But separate Frank's screw-dislocation mechanism from genuine templated copying: the screw dislocation propagates a polytype indefinitely because the step-edge *is* the template winding upward — it never creates a new independent sequence. True copy-by-contact needs flat 2D nucleation where the substrate biases incoming adatoms into registry. This works, but is entirely driven by local free-energy minimization of the material.
>
> **Q3 — Closure: is the copier on the tape?** *No. This is where the channel fails.* The "copier" is the energy difference ΔE between correct stacking and a fault. ΔE is dictated by the bulk Hamiltonian — composition, temperature, pressure, drive — **not by the sequence.** A random stacking sequence has no mechanism to alter the interatomic forces of the layers deposited on top of it. Local context might shift surface energy by a fraction of an meV but opens no programmable parameter space for the tape to invent a better replication mechanism. Fidelity is a rigid thermodynamic constant of the apparatus; improvement cannot be stored on the sequence, so pressure only sorts a standing population — it cannot incrementally build a better copier.
>
> **Q5 — Topological Z₂ and the error threshold.** *Fails for the same reason.* Kane–Lubensky isostatic lattices carry a topological polarization R_T fixed by the geometry of the unit cell — the hinge asymmetry is set by the material; a sequence of alternating polarizations cannot "learn" to align better. Bootstrapping needs N(1−q)<1, but q is capped by the Boltzmann factor of the material's defect energy and is permanently locked; if N exceeds the bath's natural q allows, information washes into an equilibrium of faults (error catastrophe). The sequence cannot evolve to raise q because it does not control q.
>
> **Summary.** You isolated a discrete state that can be copied by contact and survive separation. But it bottoms out exactly where chirality did: **the fidelity lives off the tape.** No closure → no bootstrap.

### Model c

> **This is the first stacking candidate that *does not* bottom out where chirality did. Cairns-Smith clay genes are the direct precedent.**
>
> **The pivot matters.** Chirality failed because you cannot mint a pseudoscalar from achiral grains with a rank-2 fabric tensor. Stacking parity does not have that problem: ABC vs ABA is a **discrete positional choice** in a mass-carried medium — a sphere in the dimple of the layer below has two inequivalent minima, B or C. Z₂-like, native to granular/colloidal layering.
>
> **Q1 — Discreteness + one-generation metastability: ESTABLISHED.** Discrete at the front (close-packing geometry; granular confinement+gravity selects discrete sites; colloidal — Pusey 1989, van Blaaderen). Metastable > one layer time, held by: stacking-fault energy (metals 20–300 mJ/m², SiC few meV/atom but kinetic barrier); cooperative-sliding barrier (Hirth & Lothe); in athermal grains, friction + jamming (persists until re-agitated above yield). Colloidal hard spheres: fcc–hcp ΔG ~0.001 kT/particle (Bolhuis & Frenkel Science 1997) but barrier ~ many kT → random stacking lives ~10⁴ Brownian times. SiC polytypes (3C/4H/6H/15R) coexist for geological times (Frank 1951; Krishna & Verma).
>
> **Q2 — Copy-by-contact: ESTABLISHED, distinguish two mechanisms.** (1) Epitaxial templating — a pre-existing layer's dimples impose B or C on new material by contact (colloidal epitaxy, van Blaaderen Nature 1997; MBE 6H-SiC grows 6H under low supersaturation). (2) Frank screw-dislocation mechanism — a screw dislocation with Burgers vector = n×layer spacing exposes a spiral ledge that *is* the sequence, replicating it indefinitely (Frank 1949; Mardix). This is exactly Cairns-Smith's 1966/1982 clay-gene mechanism: silicate layers replicate stacking faults by templated growth, proposed as primitive heredity.
>
> **Q3 — Closure: the gate, and where stacking also fails strong closure.** **Weak closure (adapter-free, output-is-input): PASSES.** A layer of spheres *is* its own template — no separate read-head; unlike DNA needing polymerase, a granular layer turned over can template. **Strong closure (fidelity itself on the tape, so a better-copying variant copies itself better): NOT ESTABLISHED, all precedent → NO.** Copy fidelity q = 1−p_fault is set by supersaturation, temperature, impurities, growth rate, confinement — not the sequence. Worse, a stacking fault often *lowers* the barrier for the next fault (error begets error). In Cairns-Smith tests (Weiss 1981; Bradley 1980 critique) clay faults replicated but mutation rate was high and fidelity did not evolve because improvement lived in solution chemistry, not on the clay. Selection can *sort* a standing population (e.g. a more-stable 6H spiral) but that is not *storing* improved copying on the tape. **This is the clean negative: a heritable memory channel that can be sorted, not a von-Neumann constructor whose fidelity can bootstrap. Same structure as the chirality failure, later in the chain: chirality failed at discreteness, stacking fails at evolvable fidelity.** *Rescue path (open):* give the sticks side-patches whose affinity depends on stacking parity, so a variant with a stronger correct-patch improves its own q — but that is stacking **plus chemistry**, essentially inventing base-pairing.
>
> **Q4 — Survives separation: ESTABLISHED YES, along the weak plane, with a timing constraint.** ABC vs ABA graphene survives exfoliation/cleavage/transfer (Lui Nano Lett 2011); MoS₂ 2H/3R survives exfoliation; SiC/ZnS cleavage ∥ basal plane preserves the polytype in each fragment (⊥ scrambles). Granular/colloidal: brittle fracture preserves stacking per fragment; re-fluidizing erases it → **copy must complete before separation.** Budding/pinch-off/fracture along the layer boundary is fine; crushing through the sequence is not.
>
> **Q5 — Topological mechanical Z₂ + threshold.** Kane–Lubensky (Nature Physics 2014): Maxwell isostatic lattices (z=2d) carry a quantized polarization R_T, discrete, gap-protected. But it is **polar** (a favored direction a drive can re-set across the isostatic transition), no established contact-copying in a growing jammed packing, and near point J the gap is tiny → not robust. **Weaker candidate than stacking.** Threshold: colloidal epitaxy p_fault ~10⁻³–10⁻² → N~100 before washout; granular ~10⁻²–10⁻¹ → N~10–100; Cairns-Smith needed q>0.99 for N~20, not achieved (error catastrophe was his failure mode). Screw-dislocation growth pushes effective q→1, but that stability lives in the dislocation **line** — an extended defect not encoded in the sequence — again fidelity off the tape.
>
> **Unified verdict.** Q1 PASS · Q2 PASS · Q3 FAIL-strong / PASS-weak · Q4 PASS (copy-before-separate) · Q5 topological weaker, washes out for long N. **Stacking-order gives a real, closable-weakly, above-threshold-for-short-N heritable *memory* channel that survives copy-and-separation — further than chirality — but not a bootstrappable replicator whose copier's fidelity rides on the tape. It bottoms out at "memory that can be sorted, not a constructor that can evolve to copy better."** For closure you must make the stacking-fault energy itself a heritable variable encoded in the monomer (a patchy particle whose patch strength depends on local stacking) — pure geometry alone does not close.
